## Context: Zero Fees Evolution & Why This Matters

### Business Objective

**Primary**: Increase GPV by driving merchant switching using a "0% credit card processing fees" positioning.

**Secondary**: Drive high-margin payment processing revenue, primarily through the ability to charge 3%+ on debit cards in cash discount model (even when offset by GPV reduction from increased cash usage).

The challenge: **Hard constraints** limit how "zero fees" can work. Merchant constraints: final price must be known before payment method is presented, and card type (credit vs debit) is unknown until after the customer decides to pay. Our constraints: transparency rules require customers see pricing logic upfront, and we can't use silent fallback or post-tender price adjustments.

These constraints mean "zero fees" requires **passing costs to customers** through one of three approaches we explored:

- **Model A (Credit Fee/Surcharge)**: Base price shown, fee added for credit cards only
- **Model B (Embedded Pricing)**: Fee-inclusive price shown upfront, discount offered for cash/ACH
- **Model C (Channel-based)**: Different pricing models per surface (POS, invoice, online), including convenience fees for online channels (applies to debit, credit, and ACH)

Model A is what we commonly call **surcharging**. Model B is what we're now recommending as **cash discount**. But these terms are confusing in the market, so let's define them clearly.

## Definitions: Dual Pricing vs Cash Discount

The terms "dual pricing" and "cash discount" are **sometimes used interchangeably** in the market, but they describe different merchant and customer experiences. This confusion matters because it affects how merchants understand the offering and how competitors position their solutions.

### Side-by-Side Comparison

| Dimension | **Dual Pricing** | **Cash Discount** |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **What customer sees** | **Two prices displayed**: "Cash: $100 / Card: $103" | **One price displayed**: "$103 (save $3 with cash)" |
| **What's on shelf/menu** | "$100 cash / $103 card" on every item | "$103" (discount communicated at POS or via signage) |
| **Perception** | "Card costs more" (negative, forced comparison) | "Cash saves money" (positive framing) |
| **Merchant setup** | Two prices posted for every item | One price posted (card), system applies discount at POS for cash |

### Market Confusion: Why This Matters

Some providers **market "cash discount + pricing calculator"** as "dual pricing" even though only **one price** (the card price) appears on shelves, menus, and websites. Here's why:

- **Technical truth**: The system calculates two prices behind the scenes (card price and cash price)
- **Customer experience**: Only one price is posted; cash discount is revealed at payment time
- **Regulatory framing**: Single posted price = cash discount model, not dual pricing

**For this readout**: When we say **"cash discount"**, we mean:
- Single posted price (card price, fee-inclusive)
- Cash/ACH discount applied at payment
- Merchant uses pricing calculator tool to set card prices that recover processing costs
- Customer sees "pay less with cash" messaging, not two posted prices

**Why dual pricing failed**: Merchants rejected the idea of posting two prices on every shelf, menu, and online listing. It's operationally complex, visually cluttered, and creates customer friction.

## What We Learnt: Dual Pricing Rejected, Cash Discount Tolerated

**Survey Results** (n=9: F&B, field services, pet services, varying ticket sizes):
- **7 of 9 merchants (78%) chose cash discount** on instinct; **all 7 stuck with it** after operational deep-dive
- **Dual pricing never won a single vote**
- **Zero merchants switched** after learning implementation details

**Why Dual Pricing Fails**:

1. **Visual clutter + forced comparison cheapens customer experience**: Merchants describe dual pricing as *"gobbledygook"*, *"gas station pricing"*, *"nickel-and-diming"*. Side-by-side prices force customers to compare, creating negative perception. Quote: *"a terrible, terrible option that completely is not doable"*

2. **Operational complexity**: Shelf labels and menus don't support displaying two prices per item. Even if rebuilt, visual clutter remains. Restaurant owner: *"menu boards already pack two sizes per item—no room for second price column"*

**Top two reasons merchants chose cash discount** (equal weight): **Simpler for customers** and **easier operationally**. Unusual: cash discount wins on both customer experience and operations simultaneously.

**Full research findings**: See attached study for detailed merchant quotes, operational walkthroughs, and compliance findings.

### Cash Discount: Tolerated, Not Loved

**Main takeaway**: Resistance to raising prices is the core blocker—**this applies to dual pricing too**. Zero merchants are "very comfortable" raising prices to cover card fees. Quote: *"the more you raise your prices, the higher the fees are. So it's kind of a catch-22"*

**Why tolerated**:
- Better than nickel-and-diming customers with surprise fees
- Positive framing: "Save with cash" vs "card costs more"
- Works regardless of current fee strategy (5 of 9 absorb fees today, 4 of 9 already pass through)

**Merchant workaround**: Bake price increase into routine annual reset and never name it. Positioning: *"You're probably already raising prices this year—use cash discount to claw back the card-fee piece without calling it out"*

## What We're Doing Now: Cash Discount for Targeted Segments

### Recommendation: Cash Discount as Primary Path

We recommend **cash discount** as the core offering for in-person merchants who:
1. Are blocked from effective surcharging (jurisdiction bans, debit volume, customer backlash)
2. Have mixed cash/card customer base who respond better to "discount" framing than "fee" framing
3. Need processing cost relief but can't or won't post two prices

This is a **strong recommendation with caveats**—the open questions above (Q1 and Q2) will determine how we message, which segments we prioritize, and whether we offer channel-specific flexibility.
